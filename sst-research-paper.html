Solid-State Transformers: Review, Simulation, and Analysis of Power Loss in High-Stakes Applications

Louis V. Antoine and Haley Couch

Advanced Power Electronics Laboratory | Grid Integration Research Group

Abstract

Solid-state transformers (SSTs) signify a groundbreaking evolution in power conversion technology, leveraging advanced power electronic converters and wide bandgap semiconductors like Gallium Nitride (GaN). These devices offer exceptional power density, efficiency, and operational flexibility, making them indispensable for demanding applications such as space satellites, spacecraft propulsion, and submarine power systems. This report provides a comprehensive review of SST topologies, including Dual Active Bridge (DAB), Modular Multilevel Converter (MMC), and Resonant Converter SST. Key performance metrics such as size, power rating, efficiency, harmonic content, power factor, and cost are analyzed.

1. Introduction

The escalating demand for efficient, compact, and versatile power conversion systems has spurred significant advancements in solid-state transformer (SST) technology, which is poised to replace traditional electromagnetic transformers in many applications. SSTs integrate power electronic converters with wide bandgap semiconductor materials, such as Gallium Nitride (GaN), to deliver unprecedented efficiency, reduced weight, and enhanced control capabilities.

2. Review of Solid-State Transformer Topologies

2.1 Dual Active Bridge (DAB) SST

The Dual Active Bridge (DAB) SST topology is designed for bidirectional power flow, a critical feature for applications requiring energy storage and load balancing. This topology uses two H-bridge converters and a high-frequency transformer, providing high efficiency at medium power levels.

Power Transfer Equation:
\[ P = \frac{n V_1 V_2}{2\pi f_s L_{lk}} \phi \left(1 - \frac{|\phi|}{\pi}\right) \]
where:
• V₁, V₂ are DC voltages on primary and secondary sides
• L_lk is the leakage inductance
• n is the transformer turns ratio
• φ is the phase shift angle

2.2 Modular Multilevel Converter (MMC) SST

The Modular Multilevel Converter (MMC) SST topology excels in high-voltage applications, utilizing modular construction to stack voltage levels. MMCs reduce switching losses and THD but at the cost of increased complexity.

Arm Voltage Equation:
\[ v_{arm} = \sum_{k=1}^{N} s_k v_{Ck} \]
where:
• N is the number of submodules
• s_k is the switching function (1 if inserted, 0 if bypassed)
• v_Ck is the capacitor voltage of k-th submodule

2.3 Resonant Converter SST

Resonant Converter SSTs use soft-switching techniques, such as Zero Voltage Switching (ZVS) or Zero Current Switching (ZCS), to minimize switching losses. This topology is ideal for minimizing harmonic distortion and operates with efficiency rates above 98%.

Resonant Tank Impedance:
\[ Z(j\omega) = j\omega L_r + \frac{1}{j\omega C_r} \]
At resonant frequency ω = ω_r, impedance is minimized for maximum power transfer

4. Power Loss Analysis

Total Power Loss Model:
\[ P_{total} = P_{cond} + P_{sw} + P_{gate} + P_{rad} \]
Conduction Loss:
\[ P_{cond} = I_{rms}^2 \cdot R_{DS(on)} \]
Switching Loss:
\[ P_{sw} = \frac{1}{2} V_{DS} I_{peak} (t_{on} + t_{off}) f_{sw} \]

5. Properties of Gallium Nitride (GaN)

Gallium Nitride (GaN) has emerged as a critical material for solid-state transformers due to its wide bandgap, high breakdown voltage, thermal stability, and radiation hardness.

GaN Transistor Properties (GS66508T and EPC2022)
Breakdown Voltage:
\[ V_{BR} = E_{cr} \cdot d = 3.3 \times 10^6 \cdot 20 \times 10^{-4} = 660V \]
Maximum Switching Frequency:
\[ f_{max} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{L_{parasitic} \cdot C_{oss}}} = 5.03 MHz \]
Thermal Resistance:
\[ R_{th} = \frac{t}{k \cdot A} = 0.385 K/W \]

6. Thermal and Radiative Analysis

Radiative Loss (Stefan-Boltzmann):
\[ P_{rad} = \sigma \cdot \epsilon \cdot A \cdot (T^4 - T_{env}^4) \]
For T = 350K, T_env = 300K:
\[ P_{rad} = 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \cdot 0.85 \cdot 0.1 \cdot (350^4 - 300^4) \approx 0.095W \]

8. Conclusions

Solid-State Transformers (SSTs) represent a transformative advancement in power electronics, offering compact designs, improved efficiency, and enhanced control capabilities compared to conventional transformers. By leveraging wide-bandgap materials such as GaN, SSTs achieve high switching frequencies, superior thermal performance, and radiation hardness, making them ideal for applications in space satellites, propulsion systems for spacecraft, and submarines.

Key Findings:
• DAB SSTs excel in bidirectional power flow and high efficiency
• MMC SSTs offer scalability and minimal harmonic distortion
• Resonant Converter SSTs demonstrate exceptional switching performance
• GaN transistors significantly enhance system performance
